You understand the framework. You know what foods support your microbiome. You've learned the meal templates and protocols.

But when you're standing in the grocery store, scrolling through a recipe blog, or looking at a restaurant menu, you still might wonder:

*"Is this food actually good for my gut? How does it compare to that other option? Should I prioritise this or that?"*

You don't want to consult a textbook every time you make a food decision. You need a quick, intuitive way to evaluate foods in the moment.

That's what the EatoBiotics Index is for.

It's a simple scoring system that rates foods across five dimensions that actually matter for sustainable gut health:

1. **Microbiome Support** — Does it feed beneficial bacteria?
2. **Nutrient Density** — Does it provide essential vitamins and minerals?
3. **Ease** — How practical is it to prepare and use regularly?
4. **Taste** — Do most people actually enjoy eating it?
5. **Access** — How available and affordable is it?

Each food gets a score from 1–5 in each category, for a total possible score of 25 points.

<ChapterPullQuote>
  The EatoBiotics Index isn't about creating "good" and "bad" foods. It's about having a practical tool that helps you make informed choices quickly — acknowledging that a food might be incredible for your microbiome but expensive, hard to find, and taste terrible. The best foods are the ones that score well across multiple dimensions — especially the ones you'll actually eat consistently.
</ChapterPullQuote>

- **20–25 points:** Exceptional — prioritise these foods
- **15–19 points:** Excellent — include regularly
- **10–14 points:** Good — use in rotation
- **5–9 points:** Fair — occasional use is fine
- **0–4 points:** Poor — minimise or avoid

This chapter explains how the Index works, shows you how to score common foods, and teaches you how to use it as a decision-making tool in real life.

## The Five Dimensions Explained

### Dimension 1: Microbiome Support (Scale: 1–5)

This measures how well a food feeds beneficial bacteria and supports gut health.

| Score | Level | Description | Examples |
|-------|-------|-------------|----------|
| **5** | Exceptional | High fiber (5g+ per serving), live bacteria, or very rich in polyphenols | Sauerkraut, lentils, kimchi, berries, tempeh |
| **4** | Excellent | Good fiber (3–5g), good prebiotic content or polyphenols | Broccoli, quinoa, chickpeas, apples, walnuts |
| **3** | Moderate | Some fiber (1–3g), some beneficial compounds | Brown rice, chicken with vegetables, eggs with spinach |
| **2** | Minimal | Low fiber, few beneficial compounds, not harmful | White fish, plain chicken breast, white rice |
| **1** | None/Harmful | No fiber, ultra-processed, high refined sugar | Candy, soda, processed snacks, most fast food |

### Dimension 2: Nutrient Density (Scale: 1–5)

This measures vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, and other beneficial compounds per calorie.

| Score | Level | Examples |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **5** | Extremely nutrient-dense | Leafy greens (kale, spinach), berries, sardines |
| **4** | Very nutrient-dense | Broccoli, salmon, eggs, legumes, sweet potatoes |
| **3** | Moderately nutrient-dense | Chicken breast, whole grains, most vegetables |
| **2** | Low nutrient density | White bread, pasta, white rice, most oils |
| **1** | Very low nutrient density | Candy, soda, chips, ultra-processed snacks |

### Dimension 3: Ease (Scale: 1–5)

This measures how practical the food is to prepare, store, and use regularly.

| Score | Level | Examples |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **5** | Extremely easy (no prep) | Canned beans, frozen berries, Greek yogurt, eggs |
| **4** | Very easy (under 10 min) | Fresh vegetables (wash + chop), chicken breast, canned fish |
| **3** | Moderate (15–30 min) | Dried beans (need soaking), whole grains, roasted vegetables |
| **2** | Somewhat difficult | Homemade fermented vegetables, tempeh from scratch |
| **1** | Very difficult | Homemade kimchi from scratch, natto, organ meat prep |

<ChapterCallout type="note">
**A Note on Ease Scores**

The Ease dimension rates foods based on how most people would experience them, not your current skill level. As you get more experienced, some "difficult" foods become easier for you personally. Adjust your personal Index accordingly — if you make kimchi all the time and it's effortless for you, bump it from a 2 to a 4.
</ChapterCallout>

### Dimension 4: Taste (Scale: 1–5)

This measures how appealing the food is to most people.

| Score | Level | Examples |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **5** | Universally loved | Berries, avocados, chicken, salmon, dark chocolate |
| **4** | Widely liked | Most well-prepared vegetables, eggs, yogurt with fruit, legumes |
| **3** | Neutral or acquired taste | Brussels sprouts, sardines, tempeh, kimchi (for newcomers) |
| **2** | Challenging for many | Bitter greens, liver, natto, very sour fermented foods |
| **1** | Widely disliked | Natto (for Westerners), extremely bitter or pungent items |

<ChapterCallout type="warning">
**Taste Is Subjective**

Taste scores reflect general population preferences, not universal truth. If you personally love a food most people find challenging, increase its taste score in your personal Index. If you dislike something most people enjoy, decrease it. Your personal Index should reflect *your* reality, not averages.
</ChapterCallout>

### Dimension 5: Access (Scale: 1–5)

This measures availability and affordability.

| Score | Level | Examples |
|-------|-------|----------|
| **5** | Widely available + affordable | Eggs, carrots, canned beans, frozen veg, oats, chicken |
| **4** | Widely available, moderate cost | Fresh berries, Greek yogurt, salmon, quinoa |
| **3** | Somewhat available, moderate-high cost | Organic produce, grass-fed meat, specialty fermented foods |
| **2** | Limited availability or high cost | Grass-fed organ meats, specialty items, wild-caught salmon |
| **1** | Very limited or very expensive | Rare specialty items, certain medicinal mushrooms |

Access varies by location. Adjust based on your personal situation.

## Foods to Minimise

Ultra-processed foods score high on ease, taste, and access (that's why they're popular) but fail on microbiome support and nutrient density.

The Index doesn't morally condemn these — it just makes clear they provide nothing for gut health. You don't need to never eat these. But they shouldn't be daily staples.

<ChapterCallout type="warning">
**Why Ultra-Processed Foods Score 12–16/25**

Soda, candy, chips, cookies, white bread, sugary cereals, fried fast food, processed deli meats, and pastries all score well on convenience dimensions (ease 5, taste 4–5, access 5) but score 1–2 on health dimensions (microbiome support 1, nutrients 1–2). The Index is honest about why these foods are popular — and equally honest about their trade-offs. Have them occasionally if you want. The low microbiome/nutrient scores mean they shouldn't be daily foods.
</ChapterCallout>

## Using the Index for Decision-Making

The Index isn't about creating "good" and "bad" foods. It's about making informed trade-offs.

### Scenario 1: Choosing Between Two Vegetables

**Iceberg lettuce** (Score: ~12/25) vs. **Kale** (Score: 21/25)

The Index tells you: Kale is objectively better for your gut and nutrition. But if you hate kale and love iceberg, eating iceberg is better than eating no vegetables at all.

**Best solution:** Rotate. Have kale when cooking at home. Have iceberg when convenience matters. Or try other greens — spinach (22/25) and arugula (20/25) — until you find ones you genuinely enjoy.

### Scenario 2: Choosing Protein for Dinner

**Lentils** (22/25) vs. **Chicken breast** (17/25) vs. **Grass-fed steak** (14/25)

The Index tells you: Lentils score highest (fiber + protein combination is ideal). Chicken is solid and accessible. Steak is expensive and provides no microbiome support.

**Best solution:** Make lentils your default 3–4 times weekly. Use chicken 2–3 times weekly. Have steak occasionally (once a week or less) if you enjoy it.

### Scenario 3: Choosing a Fermented Food

**Greek yogurt** (23/25) vs. **Sauerkraut** (19/25) vs. **Natto** (~15/25)

The Index tells you: Greek yogurt scores highest (great probiotic, easy, tasty, available). Sauerkraut is excellent but polarising. Natto is incredibly beneficial but very challenging for most people.

**Best solution:** If you're new to fermented foods, start with Greek yogurt (highest score, easiest entry point). Try sauerkraut once comfortable. Skip natto unless you're adventurous or already enjoy it.

### Scenario 4: Eating Out at a Restaurant

**Grilled salmon with roasted vegetables** (~20/25) vs. **Pasta primavera** (~14/25) vs. **Cheeseburger with fries** (~12/25)

The Index tells you: Salmon with vegetables is objectively the best choice. Pasta is moderate. Burger and fries score lowest.

**Best solution:** If you eat out occasionally, choose the salmon. If you rarely eat out and really want the burger, have it — one meal won't derail your progress. The Index clarifies trade-offs, but you make the final decision based on context.

## Building Your Personal Index

The Index uses general population averages. But you can customise it based on your personal situation.

### Adjusting for Your Taste Preferences

If you personally love a food most people find challenging, increase its taste score.

*Example:* Most people rate natto as 2/5 for taste. But you grew up eating it and love it → adjust to 5/5. Your personal natto score: 20/25 instead of 15/25. For you, natto should be a regular food.

If you personally dislike a food most people enjoy, decrease its taste score.

*Example:* Most people rate avocado as 5/5 for taste. But you can't stand the texture → adjust to 2/5. Your personal avocado score: 17/25 instead of 20/25. For you, skip avocados and get healthy fats from olive oil and nuts instead.

### Adjusting for Your Access

*Example:* Sauerkraut is rated 3/5 for access. But your local store doesn't carry unpasteurised sauerkraut → adjust to 2/5. You might choose kimchi or yogurt instead if they're more accessible for you.

*Example:* Wild-caught salmon is rated 3/5 for access. But you live near the coast and get fresh salmon affordably → adjust to 5/5. For you, salmon should be a staple.

### Adjusting for Your Skill Level

*Example:* Tempeh is rated 3/5 for ease. But you make tempeh dishes all the time and it's second nature → adjust to 5/5. Your personal tempeh score: 21/25 instead of 19/25.

The Index is a starting point. Customise it to reflect your reality.

## Common Questions About the Index

### "What if I disagree with a score?"

Adjust it for your personal Index. The scores provided are population averages. Your personal experience is what matters.

### "Can I use this for weight loss?"

The Index isn't designed for weight loss specifically, but it often results in weight loss because high-scoring foods are satiating (high fiber, high protein), nutrient-dense (more nutrients per calorie), and support metabolic health (stable blood sugar, reduced inflammation). People who eat mostly high-scoring foods often lose weight naturally without counting calories.

### "What about supplements?"

The Index is for whole foods. Supplements score differently: fiber supplements provide microbiome support but lack the full-food matrix. Probiotic supplements are expensive and less diverse than fermented foods. Multivitamins provide nutrients but no fiber or gut bacteria support. Supplements can fill gaps, but they don't replace high-scoring whole foods.

### "Can I eat only 20+ point foods?"

You could, but you don't need to. A diet of mostly 18–22 point foods with occasional 12–16 point foods is sustainable and enjoyable. Perfection isn't required.

### "What if healthy foods are too expensive?"

Focus on high-scoring foods that are also high-access:

- Lentils, chickpeas, black beans (dried = extremely cheap)
- Frozen berries
- Eggs
- Oats
- Carrots, cabbage, onions
- Canned fish (sardines, salmon)
- Plain yogurt

These foods score 19–23/25 and are very affordable. You can eat exceptionally well on a budget.

<ChapterCallout type="food">
**The Budget-Friendly Top Scorers**

You don't need expensive ingredients to score high on the Index. Dried lentils, canned chickpeas, frozen berries, eggs, oats, carrots, cabbage, onions, and canned sardines all score 19–24/25 and are among the cheapest foods in any grocery store. The EatoBiotics approach is affordable — the highest-scoring foods are often the least expensive.
</ChapterCallout>

## The Index as a Philosophy

The EatoBiotics Index represents a philosophy:

**Good nutrition isn't just about what's "healthy" in a vacuum. It's about what works in real life.**

A food that's incredible for your microbiome but tastes terrible, costs $30, and requires two hours of prep won't become a staple — no matter how much someone tells you it should.

The best foods are the ones you'll actually eat consistently. And those foods tend to score well across multiple dimensions:

- They support your gut
- They provide nutrients
- They're practical to prepare
- They taste good enough that you want to eat them
- They're accessible and affordable

The Index helps you find those foods. Not through restriction or judgment, but through clarity about trade-offs.

Use it as a guide, not a rule book. Let it inform your choices, not control them.

And over time, you'll find that the foods that score highest are the ones you naturally reach for — not because you're forcing yourself, but because they genuinely work for your life.
